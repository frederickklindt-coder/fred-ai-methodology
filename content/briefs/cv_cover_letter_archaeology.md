## SOURCE MATERIAL — first post draft (CV & the "no first person" rule)

### Why the "no first person" rule exists

It comes from **three overlapping origins**, all from the pre-2010 corporate-hiring world:

#### 1. The CV evolved from the "curriculum vitae" — a Latin academic document

The original CV (from European academia, 1700s onward) was literally "the course of one's life" — written **by a third party** to certify a scholar's record for university appointments. It was a credential ledger, not a self-presentation. When CVs migrated into business hiring, they kept that **certified-record voice**: declarative, factual, no narrator visible.

So "no I" wasn't a stylistic choice — it was a fossil from when someone else was supposed to be writing the document.

#### 2. The 1980s–2000s "professional formality" overlay

When CVs became standard for white-collar hiring, HR culture layered on **business-letter formality**: avoid contractions, avoid first person, avoid anything that sounds "casual." The logic: a CV is a *formal document*, like a legal filing. Formal documents don't say "I."

The actual reasoning was thin — mostly status signaling. *"I sound professional because I sound impersonal"* is the same impulse that gave us *"Per my last email…"* and *"At your earliest convenience."*

#### 3. The ATS / recruiter-scan era (mid-2000s onward) made it functional

Once Applicant Tracking Systems and high-volume recruiters entered the picture, the rule got a **practical justification**:

- Recruiters spend **6–8 seconds on first scan** (eye-tracking studies, TheLadders 2012 and 2018).
- "I" / "I'm" / "my" before each bullet **wastes 1-2 words of that scan** without adding signal.
- ATS keyword parsers don't care about pronouns — they care about nouns and verbs (Python, FastAPI, "shipped," "led").
- Stripping pronouns lets the **verb hit first**: *"Built FastAPI platform…"* parses faster than *"I built a FastAPI platform…"*

So the modern justification is: **for skim-readers and machines, pronouns are dead weight.** That part is genuinely true.

### Where the rule breaks down

The rule was built for a world where:
- Hundreds of CVs cross a recruiter's desk for one role
- The reader is a **gatekeeper**, not the decision-maker
- The CV's job is to **survive a 6-second filter**
- The applicant is **interchangeable** with 50 others

When **any of those conditions don't hold**, the rule weakens or inverts:

| Condition | "No I" rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Cold application, big company, 1000 applicants | **Strong** — survive a scan | Pronouns waste scan budget |
| Referral application, hiring manager reads it personally | **Weak** — reader hunting for a person | Voice is a feature, not a bug |
| Senior/founder/specialist roles | **Weak** — they want to know who you are | Personality compresses signal |
| Creative, marketing, founder-track, AI-frontier roles | **Inverts** — sounding like a template is disqualifying | Distinctiveness > conformity |
| Cover letter | **Always weak** — this doc is *supposed* to be your voice | Already first person |

### The actual modern best practice (2020s)

1. **Bullets under each role: never use "I"** — scan-efficiency rule still applies hard. *"Built X. Shipped Y. Reduced Z by 35%."* Verbs first.
2. **Summary section: voice is allowed, sometimes required** — only paragraph in the doc where the reader is looking for *who you are*, not *what you did*.
3. **Cover letter: always first person** — non-negotiable.

The high-end version of this rule: **the summary is the only place in your CV where personality is a competitive advantage, because it's the only section where every other applicant is sounding like a template.**

### The counterintuitive part

- The "no first person" rule was sold as **professionalism**.
- Its actual mechanism is **scan efficiency for high-volume gatekeepers**.
- For a referral hand-delivered to a hiring manager who *wants to know if you're a real human builder they'd want to work with*, scan efficiency is the wrong optimization target. **Distinctiveness** is the target.
- The folklore says *"be impersonal because it's professional."* The real rule is *"match your voice to who's reading and what they're optimizing for."*

## EXPANSION BACKLOG — other CV/cover-letter rules worth archaeologizing

Each of these is a candidate for its own carousel slide or its own post. Run the research prompt (`/prompts/research_cv_cover_letter_history_prompt.md`) to deepen any of them.

### Format & layout rules
- **"One page only" rule** — where it came from (1970s recruiter desk culture), when it stops applying (>10 yrs experience, academia, technical specialists)
- **"Times New Roman / Arial only" rule** — typography conformity as fax-era safety, now actively boring
- **"Reverse chronological order" rule** — invented by recruiters who wanted to scan for tenure, not by candidates optimizing self-presentation; functional skills CV exists for a reason
- **"Margins exactly 1 inch" rule** — Word default fossilized into a "rule"
- **The photo question** — required in Europe/LATAM, illegal-by-convention in US/UK, and why the geographic split exists
- **"No color" rule** — printer-era artifact; now PDF + screen reading inverts this for creative/design/AI roles

### Content rules
- **"Action verbs only" / power-verb lists** — origin is corporate HR training in the 1990s, became dogma when recruiters-coaching-candidates went mainstream
- **"Quantify everything" rule** — actually load-bearing; came from MBA-program career services in the 1980s, still works because numbers are scan-resistant
- **"References available upon request"** — dead phrase that survives because no one questions it; recruiters assume references exist, line wastes space
- **"Career objective" section** — fossil from the 1960s when CVs were one-size-fits-all; replaced by tailored summary, but objectives still appear on millions of CVs
- **"Hobbies & interests" section** — class-signaling artifact (golf, sailing, opera), now either gone or weaponized for personality fit
- **The skills-bar / 5-stars-out-of-5 visual** — invented by graphic designers showing off, copied by everyone, hated by every senior reader

### Cover letter rules
- **"Dear Hiring Manager"** — when the personal name is unknowable (and when it isn't, why "Dear [Name]" beats every alternative)
- **"Three-paragraph structure"** — invented in 1980s career-counseling textbooks, became universal, now reads as template-thinking
- **"Don't repeat your CV"** — actually load-bearing rule; cover letter exists to do something the CV can't (narrative, motivation, specific-role tailoring)
- **The opening line problem** — *"I am writing to apply for…"* vs the modern hook opening; why the latter works
- **Length rules** (one page) — origin and exceptions
- **Closing salutation** — "Sincerely" vs "Best regards" vs "Best" — formality decay over decades
- **"Why this company" paragraph** — when it's authentic, when it's filler, the sniff test recruiters actually apply

### Submission & meta rules
- **The PDF vs Word debate** — ATS preferences, file-naming conventions, what changed with the rise of GPT-readable parsing
- **File naming** — `Resume.pdf` vs `Frederick_Klindt_CV_CookUnity.pdf` and why the latter wins
- **The cover letter's actual readership** — surveys say 26-50% of recruiters read it; it's still load-bearing for the half who do, plus every hiring manager
- **The portfolio/Loom/GitHub link insertion** — modern artifacts that didn't exist in the rulebook, where they belong on the page, when they tip the read

### AI-era specific
- **The "AI-written CV" detection problem** — what gives away GPT-generated text, why "perfect grammar" is now a negative signal, how to write authentically in the AI age
- **The Trojan Horse appendix concept** — origin of "free work in the application" tactic (Patrick McKenzie / Jane Street / Stripe culture), why it converts hiring managers
- **The Loom video as cover letter** — emerging norm for technical/creative roles, when it works, when it backfires
- **GitHub-as-CV** — for engineers, when the README is the cover letter
